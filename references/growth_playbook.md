# YouTube Growth Playbook

Created by **Cruz Olli**

Use this reference when the user wants practical growth, upload optimization, live-stream strategy, title/thumbnail/description planning, publishing schedules, or package-to-retention forecasts.

## Growth principles

### 1. Growth is a loop, not a single upload trick

The useful loop is:

```text
topic demand -> title/thumbnail promise -> first 30s proof -> main-video satisfaction -> subscribe reason -> next-video path -> post-publish data -> next upload
```

Do not optimize one part while ignoring the next. A high-click title with a weak opening can hurt retention. A high-retention video with a vague title may never get enough impressions. A good live stream without clips, replay packaging, or a next-video path often loses its growth value after the live event ends.

### 2. The package is the pre-watch promise

Treat the upload package as:

- title
- thumbnail
- first two description lines
- playlist context
- pinned comment
- end screen
- first 30 seconds

The title and thumbnail create the click. The first 30 seconds must prove that the click was right.

### 3. Tags are secondary

Tags can help with misspellings and topic classification, but they are not the primary growth lever. Prioritize title, thumbnail, description, viewer satisfaction, and retention before spending time on tags.

## High-leverage growth methods

### Live-stream growth loop

Live can grow subscribers quickly when it has a concrete viewer promise and a post-live content path.

Best uses:

- Q&A for a topic where viewers have repeated confusion
- live diagnosis or review
- breaking or time-sensitive events
- community decision-making
- product/travel/food/event experiences where presence matters

Workflow:

```text
announce the promise -> schedule live -> collect questions -> go live -> pin next action -> redirect or point to VOD/Premiere -> cut Shorts -> publish follow-up long video -> review subscriber gain
```

Metrics to track:

- subscribers gained during live
- average concurrent viewers
- peak concurrent viewers
- chat rate
- replay views after 24h and 7d
- Shorts views from live clips
- next-video clicks from pinned comment or end screen

### Shorts support loop

Shorts should not be random fragments. They should either:

- prove the long video's core promise
- tease the most surprising moment
- answer one small viewer question
- drive viewers into a series or live event

Use 3-7 Shorts per strong long video or live stream, but avoid flooding the channel with clips that do not match the channel promise.

### Premiere and scheduled uploads

Use Premiere when:

- the first viewers matter for chat/community momentum
- the upload has a launch/event feeling
- you can promote the video before it goes live

Use normal scheduled upload when:

- the video is evergreen
- the first wave is less dependent on community presence
- you need clean post-publish data without live-event noise

### Packaging tests

When title/thumbnail testing is available, test materially different promises:

- outcome angle
- curiosity/contradiction angle
- target-viewer pain angle

Judge the winner by watch time and retention, not CTR alone.

When testing is unavailable, still prepare three packages and choose the one with the strongest match to the first 30 seconds.

## Folder-based video optimization

When the user provides a project folder, inspect it in this order:

1. `metadata/upload_package.json` for target viewer, topic, promise, title ideas, thumbnail text, publish goal.
2. `exports/first30.mp4` or `exports/full_video.mp4` for pacing, visual proof, audio, and first-value timing.
3. `transcripts/` for the spoken hook, dead zones, title-description wording, chapters, and Shorts candidates.
4. `timeline/` for cut density, titles, captions, and edit structure.
5. `thumbnails/` for mobile readability, promise clarity, and title-thumbnail complement.
6. `analytics/vidiq_keywords.csv` for search validation and adjacent keyword ideas.
7. `analytics/audience_times.csv` for publish timing.
8. `analytics/youtube_studio.csv` for baseline CTR, intro retention, and subscriber conversion.

## Publishing schedule

Use channel data first.

Priority:

1. YouTube Studio audience timing.
2. Consistent test windows for 4 weeks if the channel lacks audience data.
3. Time-zone choice based on the highest-value audience segment.
4. Live/Premiere scheduling at active windows; normal uploads can go 1-3 hours earlier.

Review at:

- 2 hours: packaging signal, notification/subscriber response
- 24 hours: CTR, traffic sources, intro retention, comments
- 72 hours: Browse/Suggested/Search split, average view duration, subscribers gained
- 7 days: series decision, title/thumbnail update decision, next-video idea

## Forecasting rules

Forecasts are ranges, not promises.

Estimate:

```text
Packaging-to-30s Conversion = CTR x First 30s Retention
```

Use channel baseline when available:

- CTR forecast starts from recent median CTR, then adjusts for topic strength, thumbnail clarity, and title specificity.
- First-30-second forecast starts from recent median intro retention, then adjusts for first value time, pacing, and promise match.
- Subscriber conversion forecast depends on whether the video gives a clear reason to follow the channel or series.

When baseline is unavailable, use conservative ranges and mark assumptions clearly.
